Adjectives

  • Definition
    Adjectives are words used to describe nouns or pronouns.
    He jumped the grey hurdles.
    He climbed the never-ending ladder.

    Adjectives answer these three questions:

    Adjectives
    What kind? We studied British literature.
    Which one? Salinger wrote four books.
    How many? This assignment was tough.

    Most adjectives come before the noun they describe or modify. ("modify" is used because they modify the meaning of the noun).
    For example: Large numbers of mosquitoes invaded the pool.

    Several adjectives can be combined to describe a noun as well.
    For example: The brave and courageous fighter pilot parachuted into enemy territory.

    On the other hand, there are some adjectives that actually come after the noun (see also participial and appositive phrases) or after the verb.

    Predicate Adjectives

    Predicate Adjectives (like Predicate Nouns) are adjectives that follow linking verbs. These adjectives refer back to the subject.

    Example:
    The younger child is usually rambunctious. (rambunctious refers back to child)
    The teacher seems strict, but he is actually very understanding.(understanding refers to teacher)

    Just like Direct Objects, Predicate adjectives answer the question "what" to the verb, only it is a Linking verb.

  • Articles

    Articles

    Articles are the words a, an, and the are unique adjectives that are only used introduce nouns.

    "The" refers to a specific item
    "A" refers to one item in a group, use only before words that start with a consonant.
    "An" refers to one item in a group, use only before words that start with a vowel.

  • Demonstrative

    Demonstrative Adjectives

    Demonstrative adjectives are the same as Demonstrative pronouns except they precede a noun. They are distinguished by singular or plural and near or far.

    For example:
    That stapler belongs in the office.

    The gifts you asked for are those boxes on the table.

    Below is a chart which illustrates the four ways demonstrative pronouns are used.

  • Comparative

    Comparative Adjectives

    Adjectives are used to compare nouns as well as describe them.

    There are two forms the adjectives take when they compare something: Comparative and Superlative.
    Comparative Adjectives compare two nouns to each other.
    They are formed by adding "-er" to the end of a one syllable adjective or placing the word "more" before multiple syllable adjectives.

    For example:
    Is that vase larger than the one in my window?

    Although winning is great, the more important aspect of sports is having fun.

    Superlative Adjectives

    Superlative Adjectives compare two or more nouns (or better stated, one noun to everything else).
    They are formed by adding "-est" to the end of a one syllable adjective or placing the word "most" before multiple syllable adjectives.

    Bill Gates was the richest man in the world.
    The Indiana Pacers have been the most successful team this year.

    More Examples:

    Adjective (Positive) Comparative Superlative
    tall taller tallest
    tark darker darkest
    Handsome more handsome most handsome
    eccentric more eccentric most eccentric
  • Irregular

    Irregular Comparative and Superlative Adjectives

    These adjectives do not adhere to the normal structure for creating the comparative and superlative forms.

    For example:

    Mike's Deli has goodsandwiches.    
    Sebastian's Sandwiches offers better sandwiches. (not gooder)    
    Arturo's Greek Deli creates the bestsandwiches.  (not goodest)

    The following five adjectives are irregular and should follow the patterns below instead of the normal rules.

    Adjective (Positive) Comparative Superlative
    good better best
    bad worse worst
    much, many more most
    little less least
