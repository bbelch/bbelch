Adverbs

  • Definition

    Adverbs are simply words that we use to describe verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs.
    For example: Carefully he crossed the tight rope.
    He skillfully navigated the river.

    Adverbs answer these five questions:

    How We thoroughly studied American literature.
    When Though he just left, he will return soon.
    Where She ran upstairs to her bedroom.
    Why (adverb clause) Because he loves to eat breakfast, Eli usually pours his own cereal in the morning.
    Under what conditions? (adverb clause) I read the paper while Eli eats his breakfast.

  • Function

    How to use Adverbs

    Adverbs may come before, between, or after the verb they describe or modify (we say modify because they modify the meaning of the verb).

    Before Recently I purchase a new hard drive.
    Between The computers in the school will have been fully upgraded before school starts.
    After I like onlyItalian spaghetti.

    Adverbs may also modify adjectives and other adverbs.Â  Most often than not they explain "how" or "to what extent"Â  Look at the following sentences and notice the words these adverbs describe.Â  Are theyÂ  verbs? adjectives? or adverbs?

    He arrived rather late for dinner. (not just late)
    "rather" modifies the adverb "late" which in turn modifies the verb "arrived"

    The food was extremely cold. (not just cold)
    "extremely" modifies the predicate adjective "cold" which in turn modifies the subject "food"

  • Comparative

    Comparative Adverbs

    Adverbs are used to compare verbs as well as describe them.

    There are two forms adverbs take when they compare something: Comparative and Superlative.
    Comparative Adverbs compare two verbs to each other.
    They are formed by adding "-er" to the end of a one syllable adjective or placing the word "more" before multiple syllable adjectives.

    For example:
    Choosing not to smoke will generally extend your life longer than the life of a smoker.
    Quitting before you start is more easily accomplished than after you start.

    Superlative Adverbs

    Superlative Adverbs compare two or more nouns (or better stated, one noun to everything else).
    They are formed by adding "-est" to the end of a one syllable adjective or placing the word "most" before multiple syllable adjectives.

    For example:
    My horseshoe landed shortest of all the throws.
    Chess requires the most acutely honed skills of any board game.

    Adverb (Positive) Comparative Superlative
    long longer longest
    soon sooner soonest
    lethargically more lethargically most lethargically
    eccentrically more eccentrically most eccentrically

    The following five adverbs are irregular and should follow the patterns below instead of the normal rules.

    Adverb (Positive) Comparative Superlative
    well better best
    badly worse worst
    little less least
    far (distance) farther farthest
    far (degree) further furthest

    Farther/Further Farthest/Furthest

    These two English words are very similar

    Farther means "more far/distant" in physical space while Farthest means "the most far/distant."

    For example: Jason ran farther than Brian.
    Which is farther, Greenwood or Martinsville?
    I prefer the room farthest from the end.
    Pluto was once considered the farthest planet from the sun.

    Further means "more far/distant" figuratively or non-physically, and can also mean "more/additional" while Furthest means "the most far/distant" figuratively.

    For example: Nothing could be further from the truth.
    Who is further along in his homework?
    That is the furthest thing from my mind.

    Further is also a verb meaning "to advance": This class will further your understanding of web design.
