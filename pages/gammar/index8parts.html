  • Nouns

    Definition

    Noun
    a word that names a person, place, thing or idea

    Examples

    Person ~ brother, judge, athlete, teacher, mother-in-law
    Place ~ neighborhood, county, city
    Thing ~ book, shoe, flower, pencil
    Idea ~ pride, skill, truth, democracy, success

    Types

    Common nouns identify any person, place, thing or idea or unnamed nouns.

    Concrete nouns identify things you can see or touch examples: car, book, movie, actor, mayor, athlete, city

    Abstract nouns identify ideas, feelings, or qualities that can not be seen or touched. examples: love, hate, courage, bravery, fear, history, truth.

    Proper nouns
    identify a specific person, place, thing or idea or named noun. examples: Ford Explorer, Lord of the Rings, Brad Pitt, Indianapolis

    Plural nouns identify one or more nouns. For most plural nouns you simply add -s, but for others you may add -es or -ies (see chart)

    Possessive nouns show ownership or possession
    To form the possessive form of a noun, the writer must add either an 's or simply an ' depending on how that noun ends.

    Collective nouns identify groups of people or things. Most collective nouns are singular; however, collective nouns can take either a singular or plural verb depending on how it is used in the sentence.
    The noun is singular when all members of the group act as a single unit, but it is plural if they do not act as a single unit.

    For example:
    (singular) The team shares the field with its opponent.
    (singular) The committee discusses their reactions.
    (plural) The committee disagree among themselves.

  • Pronouns

    Definition

    Pronoun is a word that replaces a noun

    Examples

    He likes to walk among trees.
    Pass bowl of pasta to her.
    They were happy about that gift.
    She is somebody important in this school.

    Types

    Personal Pronouns replace nouns which refer to specific people or things.  There are two types:  and subject and object

    Subject pronouns must be used as subjects (hence the name) and Object pronouns as objects. It is considered improper grammar to use an object pronoun as the subject of a sentence and visa versa.

    For example:
    I gave him the homework.

    "I" is the subject of the sentence, while "him" is the indirect object.

    Personal pronouns

    Subject

    Object

    Singular

    I, he, she, it, you

    me, him, her, it, you

    Plural

    We, they, you

    us, them, you

    A common mistake occurs with the pronouns "I" and "me"; people tend to use "I" too much because they think it sounds more correct. Look at the next example and predict which pronoun is correct.

    Between you and (I, me) those apples are delicious.

    The pronoun "me" is correct because it occupies the space of the object of the preposition.

    The pronoun "it" can be either a subject or an object

    The pronoun "you" can be either a subject or an object as well as singular or plural

    Always use subject pronouns when replacing nouns that follow the linking verb "be" because the pronoun actually refers back to the subject (predicate noun)

    "Knock, Knock!" "Who is there?" "It is I."

    or

    The boy in the picture is he.

    Antecedent is the word to which a pronoun refers.

    For example:
    Elizabeth rode the bus to the store. On her way back she walked.

    The pronouns "her" and "she" are used to replace the noun "Elizabeth"; therefore, "Elizabeth" is the antecedent of "her" and "she".

    Sometimes Adjectives

    Possessive pronouns are a pronouns that replace possessive nouns. They show ownership.

    They come in two forms: "before a noun" and "alone". When they come before a noun they are actually acting as adjectives.

    For example:
    The chair in the front room is mine.(alone)
    My chair is in the front room. (before a noun)

    Possessive pronouns

    Alone

    Before a noun

    Singular

    mine, his, hers, its, yours

    my, his, her, its, your

    Plural

    Ours, theirs, yours

    Our, their, your

    NEVER USE APOSTROPHES WITH POSSESSIVE PRONOUNS

    For example: "It's" is a contraction meaning "It is" whereas "its" is the possessive pronoun showing "it" owns something.

    Indefinite pronouns refer to a non-specific person or thing.

    Like possessive pronouns, indefinite pronouns can be used alone or before nouns.

    Most indefinite pronouns are singular; however, some are plural while others can be either.

    Always Singular

    Always plural

    Either

    Another, anybody, anyone, anything, each, either, everybody, everyone, everything, much, neither, nobody, no one, nothing, one, somebody, someone, something

    both, few, many, others, several

    all, any, most, none, some

    When the indefinite pronouns all, any, most, or some are followed by a prepositional phrase, look at the object of the preposition to determine whether or not to use a singular or plural verb.

    This is the only time you will ever refer to the object of the preposition when trying to determine subject verb agreement.

    For example:

    All of the milk is gone.
    All of the people are gone.

    Notice in the first sentence "milk" is singular therefore the singular form of "be" (is) is used while in the second sentence "people" is plural therefore "are" is used.

    Demonstrative pronouns are used to point something out or demonstrate.

    When they are used alone they are pronouns, but when used before a noun they become adjectives.

    (singular) The committee discusses their reactions.
    (plural) The committee disagree among themselves.

    Plural Nouns

    Nouns ending in:

    Add

    Example

    -s,-ss, -zz, -ch, -sh, -x

     add -es

    buses, buzzes, foxes

    -o preceded by a vowel

     add -s

    studios, stereos

    -o preceded by a consonant

     

    Usually add -es

    heroes, potatoes

    Sometimes add -s

    zeros, photos, pianos

    -y, preceded by a vowel

    add -s

    days, donkeys, keys

    -y preceded by a consonant

    usually change -y to -i and add -es

    cities, dairies, pennies

    -f or -fe

    Usually change -f to -v and add -es

    leaves, lives, calves

    Sometimes add -s

    roofs, surfs, beliefs

    Possessive Nouns

    Nouns ending in:

    Add

    Example

    Most singular nouns

    add 's

    John's books are new.

    Singular nouns ending in "s"

    add 's

    The bus's tires are flat.

    Plural nouns ending in "s"

    add an '

    The heroes' banquet is tonight at 9:00.

    Plural nouns not ending in "s"

    Sometimes add 's

    The men's room is located on the 3rd floor.
