Pronouns

  • Personal

    Personal pronouns are divided into two categories, Subject (nominative) and Object, depending on how they are used in a sentence.

    Subject Pronouns

    Subject pronouns may only be used as Subjects except after forms of the verb "be" where they are used in place of predicate nouns (nominative).

    Object Pronouns

    Object pronouns may only be used as Objects in place of Direct Objects, Indirect Objects and Objects of the Preposition.
    Examples: I, he, she, it, you, we, they, you, me, him, her, it, you, us, them, you.

    Notice "you" and "it" are listed above at least twice. "It" can be used as a singular non-gender replacement either in the subject or in the object of a sentence; whereas, "you" may be used as either a subject or an object, both singular and plural.

    Below is a chart which illustrates the difference between subject and object pronouns Remember subject pronouns must be used as subjects (hence the name) and object pronouns as objects.  It is considered improper grammar to use an object pronoun as the subject of a sentence and visa versa.

    For example:

    I gave him the homework.

    "I" is the subject of the sentence, while "him" is the indirect object.

    A common mistake occurs with the pronouns "I" and "me".  People tend to use "I" too much because they think it sounds more correct.  Look at the next example and predict which pronoun is correct.

        Between you and (I, me)  those apples are delicious.

    The pronoun "me" is correct because it occupies the space of the object of the preposition.

    • The pronoun "it" can be either a subject or an object

    • The pronoun "you" can be either a subject or an object as well as singular or plural

    • Always use subject pronouns when replacing nouns that follow the linking verb "be" because the pronoun actually refers back to the subject (predicate noun)

      "Knock, Knock!" "Who is there?" "It is I." or The boy in the picture is he.

  • Demonstrative

    Demonstrative Pronouns

    Demonstrative pronouns demonstrate or point out other nouns or pronouns. They are distinguished by singular or plural and near or far.

    Demonstrative Pronouns are also Demonstrative Adjectives, but they are used alone instead of preceding a noun.

    For example:
    That belongs in the office.

    The gifts you asked for are those.

  • Indefinite

    Indefinite

    Indefinite pronouns refer to a non-specific person, place, or thing which are acting as a group

    Like possessive pronouns, indefinite pronouns can be used alone or before nouns.

    Most indefinite pronouns are singular; however, some are plural while others can be either.

    When the indefinite pronouns "all, any, most, or some" are followed by a prepositional phrase, look at the object of the preposition to determine whether or not to use a singular or plural verb.

    This is the only time you will ever refer to the object of the preposition when trying to determine subject verb agreement.

    For example:
    All of the milk is gone.
    All of the people are gone.

    Notice in the first sentence "milk" is singular therefore the singular form of "be"  (is) is used while in the second sentence "people" is plural therefore "are" is used.

  • Reflexive

    Reflexive

    Reflexive pronouns are objects that refer back to the subject of the sentence or clause. ;Reflexive pronouns end in "-self" (singular) or "-selves" (plural).

    Examples:
    She congratulated herself on the "A" report. (Direct Object referring back to subject)

    Lucas baked himself a cake for his birthday.(Indirect Object referring back to subject)

    To remember her 16th birthday, Zoie wrote a letter to herself.(Object of the preposition referring back to subject)

    Reflexive Pronouns
    Singular

    myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself

    Plural

    ourselves, yourselves, themselves

    Intensive

    An Intensive pronoun emphasizes its antecedent (possibly not the subject);
    Intensive pronouns are simply reflexive pronouns referring to a different noun which may or may not be the subject.

    They end in "-self" (singular) or "-selves" (plural) and are the same as Reflexive. (see chart above)

    Examples:

    The caseworker made cup of coffee for the client herself.(referring back to "client")
    Lucashimself baked a cake for his birthday.(referring back to "Lucas")
    Zoie herselfwrote the letter to remember what she was like 10 years ago.(referring back to "Zoie")
  • Relative

    Relative Pronouns

    Relative pronouns introduce Relative clauses (or Adjective) clauses, a type of dependent clause that modifies another word, phrase, or idea in the main clause.

    There are five relative pronouns: that, which, who, whom, and whose, but whichever, whoever, whomever, may also be used.

    These pronouns also act as Interrogative pronouns, except "that" is replaced with "what".

    For example:
    The student who takes good notes will do fine on the test.("who" introduces the adjective clause "who takes good notes")

    Whoever took my keys needs to return them promptly.("Whoever" introduces the noun clause "whoever took my keys")

    Relative Pronouns
    Common five

    that, which, who, whom, and whose

    Others

    whichever, whoever, whomever, whosoever, whomsoever

    Interrogative Pronouns

    Interrogative pronouns introduce interrogative statements or simply put used to ask a question.

    There are five interrogative pronouns: what, which, who, whom, and whose, but whatever, whichever, whoever, whomever, may also be used.

    Interrogative pronouns may not have an antecedent, thus the need for the question.

    As stated above, these pronouns may also act as Relative pronouns, except "what" is replaced with "that".

    For example:
    Who takes good notes? ("who" introduces the question and the antecedent is unknown)

    Whatever happened to my keys? ("Whatever" introduces the question and the antecedent is unknown)

    Interrogative Pronouns
    Common five

    what, which, who, whom, and whose

    Others

    whatever, whichever, whoever, whomever, whatsoever, whosoever, whomsoever

Definition

Pronoun is a word that replaces a noun.

Pronouns refer back to another noun which should be made clear in the writing. That noun is known as the Antecedent.

Examples:
Personal ~ I, he, she, him, her, you
Demonstrative ~ This, that, these, those
Indefinite ~ anyone, anybody, anything, some, none
Reflexive ~ myself, himself, herself, itself, yourself
Relative ~ who, whom, whose, which, that
Interrogative ~ who, whom, whose, which, what

Antecedent

The antecedent of a pronoun is the word which the pronoun stands for.

Examples:
The student turned in his assignment. ("his" is the pronoun while "student" is the antecedent)
They made claims of unfair play during last week's football game. ("they" is the pronoun, but the antecedent is unclear, and is considered improper grammar.
