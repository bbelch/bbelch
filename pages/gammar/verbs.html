Verbs

  • Action

    Action Verbs

    Action verbs demonstrate an action. There are two types of Action Verb, Transitive and Intransitive.

    Transitive Verbs

    Transitive Verbs are followed by a Direct Object. For more on Direct Objects and Indirect Objects see below

    Example: The band members played their instruments really well.
    (played is the verb while instruments is the direct object)

    Direct Objects are nouns or pronouns that receive the action of the verb.

    Direct objects answer "who or what" after the verb.

    The students applauded the speaker. (Whom did they applaud? speaker)

    Without so many books occupying the shelf, you could put some pictures up to freshen the look. (Could put what? pictures)

    Once again, "occupying" and "could put"; are actions verbs while "shelf" and "pictures" are direct objects)

    Indirect Objects are nouns or pronouns for whom or to whom an action is performed.

    Indirect objects answer "to whom or for whom" after the verb.

    Olivia always bakes Thomas a cake for his birthday.
    Where as cake answers the question "what" to bake, Thomas answers "to whom" and is therefore the Indirect Object.

    Intransitive Verbs

    Intransitive Verbs are NOT followed by a Direct Object but possibly an adverb or prepositional phrase.

    Examples:

    My date to the movies paid for me to get in.
    (no direct object is present, "for me" is a prepositional phrase

  • Linking

    Linking Verbs

    Linking verbs show a state of being or "link" the subject to another word.

    Common Linking Verbs
    be (am is are was were) appear turn smell
    become look taste sound
    seem grow feel  

    Examples:

    Learning grammar seems extremely helpful for editing.
    After years of college, Megan is finally a doctor.

    Where Action verbs can be followed by a direct object, Linking verbs are always
    followed by a predicate word, either a Predicate Noun or a Predicate Adjective.

    Predicate Words

    Predicate words follow linking verbs and either describe or rename the subject.

    Predicate Noun is a noun or pronoun that follow linking verbs and refers back to the subject.

    My brother Jason is a nuclear technician. ("is" is the linking verb
    while "technician" is the predicate noun which renames "Jason")

    Predicate Adjective isa n adjective that follows a linking verb and describes the subject.

    The young basketball player appears tall for his age. ("appears" is the linking verb while "tall" is an adjective that describes "player")

  • Helping

    Helping Verbs

    Helping verbs enhance a verb, show degree, or indicate tense (time).

    Common Helping Verbs
    am, is, are, was, were, been has, have, had may, might
    shall, should can, could does, do, did, done

    Example:

    Many citizens may try escaping the devastation if Mt. Vesuvius should erupt.
    ("may" is helping the verb "try" and "should" is helping the verb "erupt")
  • Participles

    Participles

    Participles are forms of verbs. There are two types: present participles and past participles.
    Present participles end in "ing". Past participles have various endings, usually "ed".
    Below is a table showing some participles:

    Participles
    Verb Present Participle Past Participle
    bake baking baked
    sing singing sung
    watch watching watched
    google googling googled
    break breaking broken

    Participles can be used as adjectives.

    For example:
    The baking sun forced the swimmers to reach for their sunscreen.
    ("baking" is a present participle used as an adjective describing "sun")

    Already having bad luck, the misfits just gaped at the broken mirror.
    ("broken" is a past participle used as an adjective describing "mirror")

  • Tenses

    Verb Tenses

    Verbs are the only words that in the English language that change form to show tense.

    There are four types of tense, Simple, Perfect, and Progressive, Perfect-Progressive.

    Simple Tenses:
    Past tense of regular verbs is formed by adding "-ed" to the end of a verb.

    Example: He predicted he would get an "A".
    (predict + ed = predicted)

    Present tense of a verb only changes to show plurals (more than one).
    To form singular verbs, add "s" to the end of the verb (which is opposite from nouns)see chart below.

    Example: John walks to school every day.
    ("John" is singular therefore "walk" must be made singular by adding "s")

    They walk to school every day.
    ("They" is plural therefore "walk" must be made plural by leaving it alone)

    Subjects and verbs are oppositely affected by the singular/plural rules

    Future tense of a verb is formed by adding the helping verbs "shall" or "will".
    Example: Despite all the obstacles, Brian will finish his project.

    Perfect Verbs

    The perfect forms of verbs show action that has been completed either in past, present, or future.
    The perfect tense is created by using the helping verbs has, have, or had along with the past participle form of a verb

    Examples:
    Jason has painted his house since the tornado came.
    ("has painted" shows that Jason painted his house sometime in the present but it is now complete)

    Elijah had played with Legos until he discovered video games.
    ("had played" shows Elijah finished playing with Legos sometime in the past)

    Use "had" and the past participle (-ed)

    Use "has" or "have" and the past participle (-ed)

    Use "will" + "have" and the past participle (-ed)

    Elizabeth had walked home on several occasions.

    Elizabeth has walked home before.

    By tomorrow, Elizabeth will have walked home ten times

    The student had spoken to the teacher in the past.

    The student has spoken to the teacher recently.

    After conferences, the teacher will have spoken to the parents.

    Progressive Verbs

    The progressive forms of verbs show action that is, was, or will be in progress either in past, present, or future.
    The progressive tense is created by using the helping verbs am, is are, was, were, be along with the present participle form of a verb

    Examples:
    Jason was painting his house when the tornado came.
    ("was painting" shows that Jason was in the act of painting in the past.)

    Elijah is playing the latest Legos video games on his phone.
    ("is playing" shows Elijah is currently playing with Legos)

    Progressive Verb Forms

    Past Progressive

    Present Progressive

    Future Progressive

    Use "was" or "were" and the present participle (-ing)

    Use "am", "is", or "was" and the present participle (-ing)

    Use "will" + "be" and the present participle (-ing)

    Elizabeth was walking home from school.

    Elizabeth is walking home today.

    By tomorrow, Elizabeth will be walking home ten times

    The student was speaking to the teacher.

    The student is speaking to the teacher.

    After conferences, the teacher will be speaking to the parents.

    Perfect Progressive Verbs

    The perfect progressive forms of verbs show action that was in progress in the past but is
    now complete or an action begun in the past and is continuing now or in the future.

    The perfect progressive tense is created by using the helping verbs had, has, or have + been in conjunction with the present participle form of a verb

    Examples:
    Jason was painting his house when the tornado came.
    ("was painting" shows that Jason was in the act of painting in the past.)

    Elijah is playing the latest Legos video games on his phone.
    ("is playing" shows Elijah is currently playing with Legos)

    Perfect Progressive Verb Forms

    Past Perfect Progressive

    Present Perfect Progressive

    Future Perfect Progressive

    Use "had" + "been" and the present participle (-ing)

    Use "has" or "have" + "been" and the present participle (-ing)

    Use "will" + "have" + "been" and the present participle (-ing)

    Elizabeth had been walking home from school until she got a car.

    Elizabeth has been walking home all year.

    By tomorrow, Elizabeth will have been walking home for two years

    The student had been speaking to the teacher when she remembered the quiz.

    The student has been speaking to the teacher about her project

    After conferences, the teacher will have been speaking for twelve hours.

Definition

A verb is a kind of word that usually tells about an action or a state of being and is the one of two main parts of a sentence. (The other is the subject) Every sentence has a verb.

A verb, as a part of speech, is categorized in to three types: action, linking, helping; however, in English, verbs are the only kind of word that changes to show past, present, or future tense.

Examples

Action ~ read, accepted, diagnose, surf
Linking ~ is, seem, feel
Helping ~ can, shall, will, was, had
